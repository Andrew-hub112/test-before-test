Path A — Slower explanation

This route assumes the student sees many formulas as disconnected symbols and controls only a small part of differentiation. The goal is to explain each core idea in the simplest possible way, with direct steps.

1. Position, velocity, acceleration

What is position? It tells you where the object is.

x(t) means position as time changes.

What is velocity? It tells you how fast position changes.

v(t) = dx/dt

Simple meaning: if position goes up quickly, velocity is positive and large. If position goes down, velocity is negative.

What is acceleration? It tells you how velocity changes.

a(t) = dv/dt

2. How to detect change of direction

Do not look first at position. Look at velocity.

  1. Differentiate position to get velocity.
  2. Solve v(t)=0.
  3. Check the sign before and after that instant.

If velocity changes from negative to positive, or from positive to negative, the object changes direction.

3. Distance vs displacement

Displacement keeps direction. It can be negative.

Δx = x_f - x_i

Distance counts total ground covered. It is always positive.

distance = |Δx_1| + |Δx_2| + ...

4. Polar coordinates in the most basic way

In Cartesian coordinates, you say left-right and up-down. In polar coordinates, you say:

  • r: how far from the origin
  • θ: the angle

The radial velocity is just how fast the distance from the origin changes.

v_r = dr/dt

The angular velocity is how fast the angle changes.

ω = dθ/dt

5. Projectile motion in simple words

A projectile has two motions at the same time:

  • Horizontal motion
  • Vertical motion

Gravity changes the vertical motion.

v_y(t)=v_{0y}-gt

The total speed at the end is found with Pythagoras:

|v| = √(v_x² + v_y²)

Path B — Formal mechanics route

This route is unlocked only by a perfect score. It assumes a student can read formulas fluently and is expected to engage with derivation, sign analysis, and vector kinematics more rigorously.

1. Rectilinear motion with variable acceleration

For a cubic position law x(t)=t^3-4t^2+2, one obtains

v(t)=3t^2-8t, a(t)=6t-8.

This is not uniformly accelerated motion. The qualitative structure comes from the roots of velocity and the affine variation of acceleration.

The change of direction is not inferred from acceleration alone. It is determined by sign reversal of v(t).

2. Displacement and total distance

Given critical instants from the zeros of velocity, partition the time interval accordingly.

Δx_i = x(t_{i+1}) - x(t_i)

whereas total distance is

D = Σ |Δx_i|

3. Polar basis and kinematics

The orthonormal moving basis is

e_r=(cosθ, sinθ), e_θ=(-sinθ, cosθ)

Hence

r⃗ = r e_r, v⃗ = ṙ e_r + rθ̇ e_θ

a⃗ = (r¨ - rθ̇²)e_r + (rθ¨ + 2ṙθ̇)e_θ

4. Projectile motion as decoupled planar motion

v_{0x}=v_0 cosθ, v_{0y}=v_0 sinθ

With upward vertical axis, a launch below the horizontal implies v_{0y}<0.

v_y(t)=v_{0y}-gt

|v⃗| = √(v_x² + v_y²)
